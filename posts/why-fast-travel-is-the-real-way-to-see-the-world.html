Why Fast Travel Is the Real Way to See the World

Slow travel is the trendy advice nobody follows. For most people, fast travel — fewer places, deeper — beats a rushed checklist. Here's the case for moving quickly.

TL;DR: Slow travel is a luxury for people with unlimited time and money. For the rest of us, fast travel — hitting one city hard, eating where locals eat, using transit like a resident not a tourist — delivers more authentic experiences than a rushed multi-country checklist. The goal isn't to see everywhere. It's to understand somewhere.

Every travel article tells you the same thing: slow down. Stay longer. Don't rush. Experience a place the way locals do. Take a cooking class. Rent an apartment. Learn the language. Disconnect.

And every piece of that advice is either obvious, impractical, or counterproductive for most travelers working with two weeks of annual leave, a modest budget, and a genuine desire to see multiple places they may never return to.

The slow travel movement has become the wellness industry's equivalent of the "perfect morning routine" — aspirational content for people who have the privilege to execute it, presented as universal wisdom for everyone else.

The Privilege in the Pitch

To do slow travel properly, you need one or more of the following: remote work privileges, a sabbatical, retirement, wealth, or a complete absence of dependents. Most working adults have none of these.

The average American gets 10-15 paid vacation days per year. If you're spending two weeks in Europe, slow travel — truly immersing in one city for five or seven days — means seeing one city. One. For people who save all year for a once-in-a-decade trip, that math doesn't work.

The slow travel advocates who can afford to stay in Rome for a month rarely acknowledge this explicitly. They post gorgeous photos of their apartment in Trastevere and tell you that "the best way to see a city is to live it like a local." What they don't tell you is that their employer lets them work remotely from Italy for six weeks, or that they're retired, or that they built their business in a way that doesn't require their physical presence.

What Most Tourists Actually Do

The fantasy of slow travel — renting an apartment, shopping at local markets, making friends with the barista, learning to make pasta from your neighbor — requires a significant time investment before you see any returns. Most of these experiences don't emerge in three days. They emerge in three weeks, or three months.

What most tourists actually experience in a short trip: jet lag for the first two days, getting lost in transit, eating at tourist-oriented restaurants because they don't know any better, and wishing they had more time. The idea that you can parachute into a city for four days and "really know" it is itself a fantasy — one that slow travel advocates implicitly criticize while setting an even more impossible bar.

At least the tourist who admits they're a tourist is honest. The slow traveler who spends two weeks in Florence pretending to be Italian is cosplaying.

The Case for Fast Travel Done Right

Fast travel — moving through cities quickly, hitting highlights, not lingering — gets unfairly maligned. Done right, it's a legitimate approach that delivers real experiences:

It forces prioritization. When you have two days in a city, you have to decide what's actually worth your time. That discipline often produces better experiences than a languid week where you tell yourself you'll "get to everything." You won't. Nobody does.

It builds perspective. Seeing three cities in ten days gives you a baseline for comparison that one city doesn't. You understand what makes Paris different from London from Amsterdam — not just what makes one neighborhood feel particular. Contrast is its own form of knowledge.

It creates revisit appetite. If you rush through a city and love it, you have a reason to go back. The person who spent a week in Rome and felt they "got it" often doesn't return for decades. The person who rushed through and was overwhelmed by everything they missed has a list for next time.

How to Travel Fast Without Being a Tourist

The real distinction isn't slow vs. fast. It's intentional vs. passive. You can move quickly and still travel well. Here's how:

Use transit, not tour buses. The difference between seeing a city from a tour bus and riding its metro is the difference between watching a nature documentary and being in the forest. You don't need to go slow to go local. You need to stop thinking like a visitor.

Eat where people eat, not where TripAdvisor sends you. In every city, locals eat at places that look unremarkable from the outside. These are not hidden gems — they're obvious to anyone who lives there. Walk two blocks from any tourist area and eat wherever the line is half the length of the tourist spot. The food will be better and the bill will be half.

Stay central, move daily. Pick a neighborhood that's actually connected to the city, not a hotel district designed for visitors. Stay there, use it as a base, and make daily trips outward. This gives you the anchor of a home base with the variety of moving around.

Choose one thing per day that isn't in any guidebook. Not because guidebooks are bad — they're useful — but because the experiences that feel most real are usually not the ones that made it into print. Talk to a local. Walk a neighborhood without a destination. Find the street with the good bakery. These happen regardless of pace; they're about attention, not duration.

The Revisit Theory

Here's a reframe that changes how you think about travel: the goal isn't to see everywhere. It's to develop a relationship with the world that keeps you coming back.

The traveler who has been to 30 countries and spent two days in each has breadth. The traveler who has been to 5 cities three times each has depth. Which one understands those five cities better? Almost certainly the repeat visitor — not because of the hours logged, but because the first visit creates the context for the second, and the second creates the context for the third.

If you rush through Paris in four days and love it, you now have the context to spend four days in Paris next time and "get" more of it. The first fast visit is an investment, not a waste.

The Bottom Line

Slow travel is a privilege masquerading as wisdom. For most working people with finite vacation, the choice isn't between slow travel and fast travel — it's between fast travel done well and fast travel done badly.

Done well, fast travel means prioritizing ruthlessly, using transit like a resident, eating where locals eat, staying central, and accepting that you'll miss almost everything — and that that's fine.

The goal isn't to see everywhere. It's to understand somewhere. And you can do that in four days if you pay attention.
