You are not sleeping well. You have tried everything — blackout curtains, no screens before bed, magnesium glycinate already. And yet, here you are at 2AM, brain running, body tense, wondering if you are deficient in something else.

Maybe zinc. You have heard athletes take it. And you keep seeing magnesium supplements everywhere. Both claim to help with sleep. But which one actually works — and which one should you be taking?

Here is what the research actually says.

Magnesium: The Sleep Mineral

Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions in your body. It regulates GABA — the neurotransmitter that tells your brain to calm down. Low GABA equals racing thoughts, difficulty falling asleep, waking up at 3AM.

Most people do not get enough magnesium from food (it is found in leafy greens, nuts, seeds — foods most people eat less of). Stress depletes magnesium. Alcohol depletes it faster. So deficiency is common, especially if you drink regularly, have a high-stress life, or do not eat many vegetables.

The form matters. Magnesium glycinate (bound to glycine) is the best form for sleep — glycine itself has mild sedative effects and magnesium glycinate is well-absorbed without the digestive issues that magnesium oxide causes.

Zinc: The Recovery Mineral

Zinc does not directly make you sleepy. What it does: supports immune function, testosterone production, and muscle recovery. It also plays a role in melatonin production — the hormone that tells your body it is nighttime.

Zinc deficiency is less common than magnesium deficiency, but it happens. Vegetarians, heavy drinkers, and people with gut issues are more prone to it. Signs include: impaired taste/smell, slow wound healing, frequent colds, low libido.

For sleep specifically, zinc main benefit is indirect — better recovery means less soreness, lower inflammation, better hormone balance. If you are an athlete or physically active, zinc is more relevant. If you are mostly sedentary, magnesium is the more impactful supplement for sleep.

So Which One Should You Take?

If you had to pick one for sleep specifically: magnesium glycinate. The evidence for magnesium and sleep is more direct. GABA regulation is a primary mechanism of sleep onset — magnesium addresses that directly.

Zinc is more relevant if you are an athlete or physically active, getting sick frequently, have low libido or fatigue that might indicate testosterone issues, or have confirmed zinc deficiency.

Both? That is a reasonable stack. Take zinc in the morning (it can be stimulating for some people) and magnesium in the evening (30-60 minutes before bed).

What About Combining Them?

Some supplement companies sell sleep formulas with both magnesium and zinc plus other ingredients (L-theanine, taurine, glycine). This is not a bad approach — these minerals work on different pathways and can be synergistic.

Look for: Magnesium glycinate (not oxide, not citrate which can be laxative), Zinc picolinate or citrate (better absorption than zinc oxide), No melatonin unless you want it (melatonin has a quick tolerance build-up).

The Honest Con

Most people eating a reasonably varied diet are not deficient in zinc. If you eat meat, shellfish, legumes, and seeds regularly, zinc supplementation is less necessary. Magnesium deficiency is more common — especially if you stress a lot, drink alcohol, or do not eat many leafy greens.

Get bloodwork before supplementing with zinc long-term. Too much zinc can actually impair copper absorption and cause other imbalances.

The Bottom Line

Magnesium glycinate is the stronger evidence-based choice for sleep specifically. If you have sleep onset issues, racing thoughts at night, or high stress — start with magnesium.

Add zinc if you are an athlete, frequently sick, or have signs of zinc deficiency. Take them at different times of day (zinc morning, magnesium evening) for best effect.
